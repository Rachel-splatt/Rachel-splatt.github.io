MyMenu

This was a personal project I did to better familiarize myself with the process of designing a user experience. I thought it would be helpful to go through the steps of the design process on my own, to build a wholesome view of what user experience entails. The app I designed is a menu app that tailors a restaurant menu to a users dietary restrictions and preferences. View the InVision project.

discoveries:

At this point, I realized the scope of people this could reach was larger than just those with dietary restrictions. I would also want to consider people with food allergies, or those dieting-- factors that also result in significant menu changes. My next step was diving into understanding these people.

understanding my users:

to understand the lives of those with food allergies, restrictions, and specific preferences, I went to straight to the internet. I found communities and resources on places like reddit, discussion forums, and blogs. Here are some of the best insights I found:

the emotional aspect

As it turns out, there are real emotions associated with different dietary restrictions. People mentioned feeling embarassed about their choices, and others mentioned feeling like an annoyance, having to constantly ask waiters questions and make specific requests.

I realized that someone's diet can be a very personal thing, and this doesn't always mesh well with the social setting of a restaurant.

“It's just embarrassing when they have to cater to my food preferences though or if we go out to a restaurant and I have to ask the waiters to leave off cheese or chicken or whatever”

“I get embarrassed when people realize I'm trying to lose weight.”

"I already feel like a tool because I have clinically certified food allergies which has left me somewhat bitter about the cruel hand dealt to me, but I do like to feel as if I'm important enough to have a proper menu printed for me.”

Many articles I found just affirmed how challenging this lifestyle can be:

"5 tips to stay social when you have food allergies"

"The Embarrassment Factor"

These articles contained long lists of suggestions for what those with restrictions can do, like call ahead, find restaurants online, or just stick to the same meals from the same places.

user stories:

I took my user research and created user stories using the format As ______, I need ______.

Then, I mapped my user stories to specific features my app would need to include.

paper prototyping:

I started by designing the overall structure of my app with help from this article on information architectures in mobile applications.

Then I made paper prototypes of different screen ideas based on my user stories.


user testing:

I inputted my paper prototypes into InVision for user testing. I once read testing on paper prototypes or unfinished mockups leads to more honest feeback, plus, I wanted to challenge myself to focus on the interactions of my application and not just the design.

For this personal project, I only tested two tasks on two different users, to get some practice. That being said, I think theres a lot more information I could gain from more extensive testing. The tasks I tested for were:

  • editing the user profile to monitor "fat" under 40 grams
  • visiting the panera bread menu and finding a meal for someone with peanut allergies

turning results into design decisions:

  • Based on user testing, I know I needed to create a more organized scheme for dietary choices in both the filter options and user profile. The experience was not consistent in the two areas which caused some confusion. I decided to use tags. That way, users could search specifically for their preferences, and wouldn't be littered with a bunch of options that don't apply to them.
  • The filter adder screen presented too much information to the user, I needed to simplify this.
  • The distinction between an allergy, restriction, and moderation was a bit confusing for the users. I color coded these to make this distinction more apparent.
  • Based on user stories, it was important for my app to be convenient for one time use, but I needed to find a way to make this experience consistent with creating a profile. I could tell during my user testing this was a bit confusing as well. They didn't understand why there were two different ways to add restrictions. I decided filters added in my profile would automatically populate when a user viewed a menu, but the user would be able to add and remove tags after the fact as well.
  • For an app thats intended use was in a restaurant setting, I intentially chose a dark color scheme for lower lighting.
